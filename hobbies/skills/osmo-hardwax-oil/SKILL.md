# OSMO Hardwax Oil Finishing

Apply OSMO hardwax oil to woodworking project components (cabinet doors, frames, furniture). Osmo penetrates wood fibres rather than building a surface film, so the final result mirrors whatever sanding prep you leave behind.

## Sanding Preparation

**Grit progression — never skip steps.** Final grit determines visible finish quality under angled light.

| Stage | Grit | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| 1 | 80–100 | Remove mill marks, flatten surfaces |
| 2 | 150 | Clean up previous grit scratches |
| 3 | 180–220 | Final prep before oil (furniture-grade = stop at 220) |

- **Minimum viable:** 100 → 150. Visible micro-scratches will persist under good light, especially on plywood and light-coloured wood.
- **Furniture grade:** 100 → 150 → 180 → 220. This is what OSMO itself recommends. Gives the deep, glassy reflection look.
- Each grit pass removes roughly 50–100 microns. Clean thoroughly between passes (vacuum + tack cloth) to avoid embedding grit.

## Raising the Grain

Wiping with a damp cloth mid-sanding (after reaching ~180 grit, before final pass):

1. Dampen surface with clean cloth
2. Let dry completely
3. Touch up with 180–220 grit

Why: fresh-exposed wood fibres swell when wetted by the remaining moisture in oil solvents. Raising it early prevents post-oil fuzziness. **Essential for solid wood frames; skip on plywood veneers** — thin veneer can be damaged by over-wetting and doesn't raise grain as noticeably.

## Plywood-Specific Behaviour

Plywood panels consistently feel rougher than solid hardwood at the same grit due to:

1. **Veneer manufacturing texture** — rotary-peeled face veneers have inherent waves/height variation across the sheet that fine sanding alone cannot flatten without removing more than the veneer thickness.
2. **Growth ring variation within the thin veneer** — earlywood (soft) and latewood (hard) create micro-ridges at 150 grit as soft spots wear faster.
3. **Edge fuzz** — 6mm plywood edges expose ultra-thin end-grain layers that fray during sanding, feeling fuzzy along the perimeter.

For comparable feel to solid wood: go past 150 to at least 220 grit on plywood panels.

## Species-Specific Colour & Absorption

- **Red Grandis (and similar tropical hardwoods):** starts very pale — often white-ish or light pink when first sanded and raw. The rich reddish-brown only develops through oil penetration plus oxidation over the following days/weeks. Don't worry if colour looks wrong before the first coat; it will look correct after coat 2.
- **Maple plywood / birch face veneer:** non-porous surface with no grain channels for oil to sink into. Oil sits on top rather than absorbing, making it appear dark immediately but leaving a slick/tacky feel if not buffed off aggressively. Requires more vigorous wiping than solid wood.
- **Oak / ash:** highly porous — suck in oil fast, appear lighter as the oil disappears into grain channels. Natural colour deepens to golden/warm brown.

## Application Workflow

### Surfaces to Oil

Oil every exposed surface and edge. Rule of thumb: **if you can see it or touch it with your fingernail, oil it.**

| Surface | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Both faces of doors/panels | ✅ Oil both sides |
| All edges (top/bottom/left/right) | ✅ Oil, especially bottom edge near splash zone. 2+ coats if possible. |
| Corner joint areas between frame members | ✅ Let brush-wipe penetrate corners |
| Tenon shoulders flush against stile/rail | ✅ Touch up exposed bits |
| Panel groove (hidden channel) | ⏭️ Skip — hidden behind panel once assembled |

### Drying Setup

**Vertical is better than flat:** no sags, less dust contact on faces.

When you lack sawhorses or drying stands:

- **V-notch scrap method:** Cut shallow V notches at one end of two scrap pieces. Lay them apart on protected floor. Stand doors between notches, adjust spacing for upright hold.
- **Brick/concrete base:** Heavy bricks as bases, plank bridged across as shelf. Keeps off damp surfaces.
- **Chair & plank (indoor emergency):** Two stacks or chairs back-to-back with plank bridging, doors laid across like ladder rungs. Use rubbery material underneath to prevent sliding.

Requirements: lift 2–3cm off the ground for airflow underneath, space 2–3cm apart between pieces, protect walls/floor from drip transfer through OSMO (cardboard acts as barrier).

### Cured Surface Skins

OSMO is a hardwax oil that cures via oxygen reaction. A closed container with headspace develops a polymerised rubbery skin on the exposed surface layer:

- **Skim it off** and use the remainder — the cured skin is just already-cured material; the liquid underneath has been protected from air exposure.
- Stir well before use (wax component settles).
- For long storage: fill container closer to top to reduce headspace, or line opening with cling film before closing.

### Removing Excess Oil (Coat 1 & 2)

After brushing on oil, **wait ~10–15 minutes** before wiping off excess. The wood reaches its maximum darkness when it has absorbed all it can at that stage — this is your cue. Then use a clean cloth to **buff vigorously** until the surface feels velvety/dry, not slick or tacky.

- If your finger leaves a smear mark after 20 minutes, you wiped off too little. Go back with a fresh cloth section and take more off.
- **Porous woods** (oak, ash, etc.) suck oil in fast — they look lighter sooner as the oil disappears into the grain.
- **Non-porous woods** (maple plywood veneer, beech) hold oil on the surface because there are no pores for it to sink into. They appear dark immediately and require more aggressive buffing to remove. Failure to wipe thoroughly leaves a permanent tacky/greasy feel.
- **End grain/edges** drink up 3–5× more oil than face grain. Buff them down harder so they don't drip during drying.

### Timing Between Coats

Touch-dry: 4–6 hours (at room temperature).
Recoat window: 16h–48h is the ideal window. If longer than 48h passes between coats, lightly scuff-sand (240–320) before the next coat for adhesion.

Two coats total for furniture-grade finish. Light sanding (240 or steel wool) between coats for extra smoothness.

## Pitfalls

- **Oiling too-fine:** sanding to 320+ between coats is fine, but DO NOT leave the initial surface at 320+. OSMO needs open wood fibres to penetrate — overly fine sanding blocks absorption and creates a patchy finish.
- **Stacking doors face-to-face while wet:** they will stick together permanently. Leave each piece with exposed faces.
- **Plywood edges untreated:** the exposed edge of a thin veneer is the weakest point for moisture ingress. Always oil panel edges, even if hidden inside a cabinet carcass.
- **Using linty cloths on face grain:** always do a quick wipe across your knee or scrap wood before starting to check that your microfibre doesn't shed. If it leaves fuzz balls, switch to an old cotton t-shirt or a lint-free microfibre version (looks smoother/thicker).
- **Oil-soaked rags in the bin:** rolled-up oil-soaked cloths are a fire hazard and landfill risk. Roll them in a metal tin until fully dry or washable before disposal.

## Cloth & Cleaning Management

**Tack cloth vs microfibre:** microfibre traps surface dust via static/friction but misses dust lodged inside open pores. A tack cloth has sticky resin that pulls embedded pore dust — ideal for porous woods like Red Grandis, which look chalky/powdery after sanding because fine dust fills the large grain channels. If you don't have a tack cloth, dampen one corner of your microfibre with water and wipe across the surface to pull dust from pores; let dry completely before oiling.

**Cleaning oil-coated microfibre:**
1. Squeeze excess oil into cardboard or an old tin — don't rub first.
2. Soak in warm water (40°C) with dishwashing liquid for 10–15 minutes. Soap breaks down the oil so fibres release it.
3. Machine wash at 40°C or hand-rub under warm running water until water runs clear. Squeeze, don't wring/twist.
4. Air dry flat or hang low-heat/air only in dryer.

**Never:** use fabric softener (permanently coats fibres and kills grip), bleach (degrades structure), or wash with cotton towels (lint clogs pores). If a cloth never comes back clean, cut it up as shop rags before recycling.
